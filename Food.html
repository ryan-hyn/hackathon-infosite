Food Waste

Food waste is food that is discarded or not consumed, even though it is still safe to eat.

This matters because...

In the U.S., about 40% of food is wasted, costing $218 billion annually.

Overproduction: Often, food is produced in excess, leading to unsold goods that eventually get discarded due to spoilage or expiration.

Retail and Consumer Behavior: In supermarkets, products may be discarded due to imperfections (such as bruises or blemishes), and consumers often purchase more than needed, leading to food waste at home.

Date Labels: Confusion around labels like "sell by" or "use by" can lead to perfectly good food being thrown away prematurely.

Why does this matter?

Food waste matters because it affects nearly every part of our lives. For example,

  1. Resource Depletion: Wasting food also means wasting the natural resources used to produce it, such as water, energy, and land.
  2. Economic Costs: Food waste leads to significant financial losses for consumers, businesses, and governments. Wasting food also means wasting the labor and resources that went into its production, transportation, and storage.
  3. Food Insecurity: At the same time, millions of people around the world face food insecurity. Reducing food waste could help alleviate hunger by redirecting surplus food to those in need.
  4. Environmental Impact: Food waste contributes to environmental degradation, particularly through the production of methane in landfills.

What are things that I can do to help?

Plan Meals and Shop Smart: Plan meals ahead of time, make shopping lists, and stick to them to avoid overbuying.

Understand Food Labels: Pay attention to "use by" and "sell by" dates.

Compost: Instead of throwing away food scraps, compost them to return nutrients to the soil and reduce landfill waste.
